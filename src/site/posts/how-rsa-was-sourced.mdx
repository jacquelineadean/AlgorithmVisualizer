Every step of the [RSA visualization](#/visualizer/rsa) resolves at least one
citation, or the test suite fails. This post is the promised companion: what
each source actually contributes, and where the visualization knowingly
simplifies.

## The primary source

**Rivest, Shamir & Adleman (1978)**, *A Method for Obtaining Digital Signatures
and Public-Key Cryptosystems*, is the backbone — eleven of thirteen steps cite
it. The key-generation act follows §VII: primes, the modulus, the totient, and
the private exponent derived with Euclid's algorithm. Two details from the paper
that most retellings drop:

- The paper chooses **d first** and derives e; practice later flipped the order.
  The "pick the public exponent" step says so rather than pretending the modern
  convention was always there.
- §VI proves correctness for **every** message m, not just those coprime to n —
  the "why decryption inverts encryption" step leans on Euler's theorem for the
  clean case and points at the paper for the general one.

## The bedrock and the context

**Euler (1763)** supplies the totient theorem the proof stands on, and
**Diffie & Hellman (1976)** the public-key idea RSA realized — the "publish
(e, n)" step cites both, because publishing a key *was* the radical act.
**Knuth's** §4.5.2 and §4.6.3 back the two working algorithms on screen:
extended Euclid and square-and-multiply.

## The modern layer

**RFC 8017** marks every place practice has moved: λ(n) instead of φ(n),
e = 65537, and — most importantly — OAEP padding, cited on the encoding step
where the visualization's character-by-character encryption would otherwise
look like advice. **NIST SP 800-57** sets the key sizes that make the toy
primes a labeled teaching device, and **Katz & Lindell** carry the
textbook-RSA-is-not-a-scheme warning.

## The deliberate deviations

All tagged `pedagogical` in the UI, all with the honest version cited inline:
two-digit primes, per-character ECB-style blocks, and printable-ASCII encoding
in place of the paper's two-digits-per-letter scheme. The rule is not that the
visualization never simplifies — it is that a simplification never passes
itself off as the algorithm.
